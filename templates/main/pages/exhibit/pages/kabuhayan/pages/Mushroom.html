Mush-Room for Improvement: Establishment of Mushroom Production for the livelihood of the Indigenous People of Bangkal, Bataan

>
Nutritive and health-promoting value of mushrooms.

The Indigenous People (commonly called Aeta) represent nearly 14% of the countryâ€™s population. They are among the poorest and the most disadvantaged ethnic group in the country. Illiteracy, unemployment, and incidence of poverty are much higher among them than the rest of the population. IP settlements are remote, without access to basic services, and are characterized by a high incidence of morbidity, mortality, and malnutrition. In this endeavor, the Bataan Peninsula State University in partnership with different agencies assisted the community through research, production, and extension services. One of the regular extension activities offered by the Abucay campus of BPSU is the promotion of mushroom specifically oyster mushrooms and rice straw/banana mushroom and their economic importance.

The DOST-III through its Community Empowerment through Science and Technology (CEST) Program, provided appropriate technology and a mushroom production facility and processing to provide a livelihood program for the IPs in Bangkal, Abucay. Eleven (11) Aetas along with their community chieftain attended the capacity building conducted at the Central Luzon State University last September 19 & 20, 2019. In addition to that, an Interactive Multimedia Audio Visual Teaching Aids was also given to the Samal National High School Annex Bangkal Extension. At present there are currently five (5) members who goes to the BPSU to continue their mushroom processing even with the on-going pandemic.
